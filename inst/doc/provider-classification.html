1. Overview

A mystery-caller study begins with a provider roster: a list of physicians whose appointment-scheduling behaviour will be tested. Before any statistical model can be estimated, that roster must be enriched with demographic, geographic, and practice-setting variables. Raw data from the National Provider Identifier (NPI) registry and related sources are not analysis-ready on their own — field names are cryptic, geographic codes are numeric, and key variables like physician age and gender are often missing or incomplete.

This vignette walks through every enrichment step implemented in mysterycall, from the initial NPI search through to a Table 1-ready data frame.

The enrichment pipeline

Raw NPPES data (CSV / API)
        |
        v
[1] validate_and_remove_invalid_npi()   -- drop deactivated or duplicate NPIs
        |
        v
[2] mysterycall_classify_practice_setting()   -- Academic / Government / Private
        |
        v
[3] Join USDA RUCA crosswalk                  -- attach ruca_code to each ZIP
        |
        v
[4] mysterycall_classify_ruca()               -- Urban / Suburban / Rural
        |
        v
[5] mysterycall_assign_region()               -- Census / ACOG / AAO-HNS region
        |
        v
[6] mysterycall_classify_medical_school()     -- US_MD / US_DO / CAN_MD / IMG
        |
        v
[7] mysterycall_impute_age()                  -- estimated age from grad year
    mysterycall_age_category()                -- decade brackets for Table 1
        |
        v
[8] mysterycall_genderize()                   -- API gender backfill for ~5% missing
        |
        v
[9] mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars()         -- standardise and combine all vars
        |
        v
[10] Analysis-ready data frame

Each step is independent enough to be run on its own, but the order matters: you cannot classify RUCA codes until you have joined the crosswalk, and you cannot call mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars() until you have raw gender values to standardise.

Target audience: Physician researchers who will execute this code themselves. No prior experience with the NPPES API or USDA geographic data systems is assumed.

2. Practice setting classification

What is practice setting?

In mystery-caller research, practice setting is one of the strongest predictors of appointment availability and wait time. Academic medical centres have complex scheduling systems, residents who share patient loads, and often longer wait times. Government facilities (VA, military) operate under federal mandates and may have different referral patterns. Private practices behave differently again.

The function mysterycall_classify_practice_setting() assigns each provider to one of three categories: "Academic", "Government", or "Private Practice". It also returns "Unknown" (or your custom na_label) when the input is NA or blank.

The three-tier hierarchy

Classification is performed in a fixed priority order:

Government  (checked first)
    |
    v
Academic    (checked second)
    |
    v
Private Practice  (default when no pattern matches)

Government is always checked first. This is a deliberate design decision. Consider “VA Medical Center Denver”: the substring "medical center" appears in the academic pattern list because most facilities with that phrase in their name are teaching hospitals. But the VA is a federal government facility, not an academic one. Checking government patterns first ensures that VA and military facilities are never mis-classified as Academic, regardless of what other words appear in their name.

If a name matches no government or academic pattern but is non-empty, it is classified as "Private Practice". This is the correct default for the vast majority of physician names in NPPES, which tend to be short strings like "Smith ENT Associates LLC" that contain no institutional keywords.

Built-in patterns

mysterycall ships with two accessor functions that let you inspect — and extend — the built-in keyword lists without having to look at the source code.

# View all built-in academic patterns (40+ keywords)
mysterycall_academic_patterns()

The built-in academic list includes broad institutional keywords like "university", "medical center", "teaching hospital", and "health sciences center", as well as named institutions that are academically affiliated even when the word “university” does not appear in their name: "mayo clinic", "cleveland clinic", "johns hopkins", "memorial sloan", "md anderson", and flagship programmes at "duke", "stanford", "ucsf", "yale", "upmc", "vanderbilt", and dozens more.

# View all built-in government/military patterns
mysterycall_government_patterns()

The government list matches "va " (note the trailing space, to avoid matching “Nevada”), "veterans", "dod ", "department of defense", "military", "army", "navy", "air force", "walter reed", "tricare", "indian health", "ihs", "federal", "county health", and "public health".

All pattern matching is performed against the lowercase version of the facility name, so you never need to worry about whether your data uses title case, all caps, or mixed case. The function calls tolower() internally before any pattern comparison.

Extending vs replacing patterns

You have two options when the built-in lists do not cover your study population:

Option 1 — Extend: Keep all built-in patterns and add your own. This is the recommended approach for most researchers.

# Add "regional medical center" to the academic list without losing any built-ins
my_academic <- c(mysterycall_academic_patterns(), "regional medical center", "academic health")

# Add a state health department abbreviation to the government list
my_gov <- c(mysterycall_government_patterns(), "state health dept", "chc ")

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    practice_setting = mysterycall_classify_practice_setting(
      org_name,
      academic_patterns   = my_academic,
      government_patterns = my_gov
    )
  )

Option 2 — Replace: Supply your own complete pattern vectors. Do this only if your study uses a highly specialised provider population (e.g., only correctional health facilities) where the built-in patterns are likely to produce false positives.

# Replace academic patterns entirely with a minimal set for a corrections study
corrections_academic <- c("prison hospital", "correctional medical center")

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    practice_setting = mysterycall_classify_practice_setting(
      org_name,
      academic_patterns = corrections_academic   # government_patterns uses built-in default
    )
  )

Real-world classification examples

The following examples illustrate how the hierarchy resolves ambiguous cases:

test_names <- c(
  "University of Colorado Medical Center",  # Academic: "university" and "medical center" match
  "VA Medical Center Denver",               # Government: "va " matches before "medical center" is tested
  "Denver Women's ENT Associates LLC",      # Private Practice: no academic or government match
  "Johns Hopkins Medicine",                 # Academic: "johns hopkins" in built-in list
  "Walter Reed National Military Medical Center", # Government: "walter reed" and "military" match
  "Vanderbilt-Ingram Cancer Center",        # Academic: "vanderbilt" and "cancer center" match
  "",                                       # Unknown: blank input
  NA_character_                             # Unknown: NA input
)

mysterycall_classify_practice_setting(test_names)
#> [1] "Academic"         "Government"       "Private Practice"
#> [4] "Academic"         "Government"       "Academic"
#> [7] "Unknown"          "Unknown"

The na_label parameter controls what is returned for NA and blank inputs. The default is "Unknown", but you may prefer NA_character_ for downstream is.na() checks:

mysterycall_classify_practice_setting(NA_character_, na_label = NA_character_)
#> [1] NA

Applying to a full roster

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    practice_setting = mysterycall_classify_practice_setting(org_name)
  )

# Check the distribution — expect Private Practice to dominate
table(roster$practice_setting, useNA = "always")

A typical otolaryngology roster might look like:

Academic         Government   Private Practice        Unknown
      42                 8              247              3

If you see more than 5% "Unknown", it usually means a significant share of providers have NA in the org_name field. Investigate with filter(is.na(org_name)) before proceeding.

Common mistake: case sensitivity

mysterycall_classify_practice_setting() applies tolower() to facility_name internally before pattern matching, so "UNIVERSITY OF COLORADO", "University of Colorado", and "university of colorado" all produce "Academic". You do not need to pre-process the case of your input column. If you supply your own academic_patterns or government_patterns, write them in lowercase — they are matched against the lowercased name, so an uppercase pattern like "University" will never match.

3. Urban/Rural classification with RUCA codes

What are RUCA codes?

Rural-Urban Commuting Area (RUCA) codes are a geographic classification system developed by the USDA Economic Research Service (ERS). They assign a numeric code to each US ZIP code based on the population density of the area and the proportion of workers who commute to an urban core. RUCA codes are widely used in health services research to characterise provider practice locations on an urban-to-rural continuum.

RUCA codes range from 1 to 10:

Code Description
1 Metropolitan area core: primary flow in urban area
2 Metropolitan area high commuting
3 Metropolitan area low commuting
4 Micropolitan area core
5 Micropolitan high commuting
6 Micropolitan low commuting
7 Small town core
8 Small town high commuting
9 Small town low commuting
10 Rural areas

mysterycall maps these ten codes to three clinically meaningful tiers:

RUCA range Category
1–3 Urban
4–6 Suburban
7–10 Rural

This three-tier mapping is the standard used in most healthcare access studies and by the Federal Office of Rural Health Policy.

Where to get the crosswalk file

The USDA ERS publishes a ZIP-to-RUCA crosswalk at:

https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/rural-urban-commuting-area-codes/

Download the ZIP code file (not the census tract file). As of 2023, the file is named something like ruca2010revised.xlsx. Export it to CSV. The two columns you need are the ZIP code column (typically ZIP_CODE) and the primary RUCA code column (typically RUCA1).

Step 1: Load and clean the crosswalk

library(readr)

ruca_crosswalk <- read_csv(
  "data-raw/ruca2010revised.csv",
  col_types = cols(
    ZIP_CODE = col_character(),  # keep leading zeros: "01234" not 1234
    RUCA1    = col_double()
  )
) |>
  dplyr::select(zip5 = ZIP_CODE, ruca_code = RUCA1) |>
  dplyr::distinct(zip5, .keep_all = TRUE)  # one row per ZIP

Critical: Always read ZIP codes as character, not integer. ZIP codes like 01234 (Massachusetts) will lose their leading zero if read as numeric, causing all New England providers to fail the join.

Step 2: Prepare the ZIP column in your roster

NPPES exports ZIP codes in a column named addresses_postal_code. That column often contains 9-digit ZIP+4 codes (e.g., 80204-2127). You need the 5-digit base ZIP only.

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    zip5 = stringr::str_sub(addresses_postal_code, 1L, 5L)
  )

Step 3: Join the crosswalk using mysterycall_safe_left_join()

roster_with_ruca <- mysterycall_safe_left_join(
  left         = roster,
  right        = ruca_crosswalk,
  by           = "zip5",
  label_left   = "roster",
  label_right  = "USDA RUCA crosswalk",
  min_coverage = 0.90   # warn if >10% of providers have unmatched ZIPs
)

mysterycall_safe_left_join() performs several safety checks automatically:

  • It asserts that ruca_crosswalk has unique keys on zip5 (no duplicate ZIP rows that would fan out your roster).
  • It coerces mismatched column types (e.g., if one side has zip5 as integer and the other as character) to prevent silent zero-match joins.
  • It reports join coverage: what fraction of roster rows found a matching ZIP in the crosswalk.
  • It stops with an informative error if coverage falls below min_coverage.

If coverage is below 90%, inspect the unmatched ZIPs:

unmatched <- dplyr::anti_join(roster, ruca_crosswalk, by = "zip5")
table(unmatched$addresses_state)

Post office box ZIP codes, US territories (Guam, Puerto Rico, US Virgin Islands), and military APO/FPO addresses are the most common sources of unmatched ZIPs. Decide in advance whether these providers should be excluded from the RUCA analysis or assigned a manual code.

Step 4: Classify RUCA codes

roster_with_ruca <- roster_with_ruca |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    geography = mysterycall_classify_ruca(ruca_code)
  )

table(roster_with_ruca$geography, useNA = "always")

Ordered factor for ggplot2 and ordinal models

When using RUCA category as an outcome in an ordinal logistic regression or as an axis in a ggplot2 bar chart, you want the levels to appear in geographic order (Urban < Suburban < Rural), not alphabetical order. Use as_factor = TRUE:

roster_with_ruca <- roster_with_ruca |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    geography_f = mysterycall_classify_ruca(ruca_code, as_factor = TRUE)
  )

levels(roster_with_ruca$geography_f)
#> [1] "Urban"    "Suburban" "Rural"    "Unknown"

# Now ggplot2 and MASS::polr() will use the correct order automatically

Custom breakpoints

The default cutpoints (1–3=Urban, 4–6=Suburban, 7–10=Rural) follow USDA ERS convention, but some journals and federal agencies use a two-tier (Metro vs Non-Metro) or an alternative three-tier system. You can change the thresholds with urban_max and suburban_max:

# Two-tier: Metro (RUCA 1-3) vs Non-Metro (RUCA 4-10)
roster_with_ruca <- roster_with_ruca |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    geography_2tier = mysterycall_classify_ruca(
      ruca_code,
      urban_max    = 3,
      suburban_max = 3,   # no Suburban tier; anything >3 is Rural
      labels       = c("Metro", "Metro", "Non-Metro"),  # labels[1] and [2] collapse
      na_label     = "Unknown"
    )
  )

Actually, for a strict two-tier system the cleanest approach is:

roster_with_ruca <- roster_with_ruca |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    geography_metro = dplyr::case_when(
      is.na(ruca_code)  ~ "Unknown",
      ruca_code <= 3    ~ "Metro",
      TRUE              ~ "Non-Metro"
    )
  )

Coverage check

Always report the fraction of providers with missing RUCA codes in your methods section:

n_total    <- nrow(roster_with_ruca)
n_missing  <- sum(is.na(roster_with_ruca$ruca_code))
pct_missing <- round(n_missing / n_total * 100, 1)

message(sprintf(
  "%d of %d providers (%.1f%%) had no RUCA code (unmatched ZIP).",
  n_missing, n_total, pct_missing
))

A missing rate above 5% should trigger a manual review of the unmatched ZIP codes.

4. Census region assignment

Which system should you use?

mysterycall_assign_region() supports three geographic classification systems. The right choice depends on your study population and the expected readership:

System Use when
"census" General US population health research; Census Bureau regions (Northeast, Midwest, South, West) are recognised by all audiences
"acog" OB/GYN studies; ACOG Districts I–XII align with ACOG committee structure and are cited in obstetrics journals
"aao_hns" Otolaryngology studies; AAO-HNS Districts 1–8 align with the specialty’s committee structure

For a multi-specialty mystery-caller study, use "census". For a study submitted to Obstetrics & Gynecology or AJOG, use "acog". For a study in Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery or Laryngoscope, use "aao_hns".

All three systems with examples

states <- c("CO", "Texas", "New York", "california", "Massachusetts", NA)

# US Census Bureau regions
mysterycall_assign_region(states, system = "census")
#> [1] "West"      "South"     "Northeast" "West"      "Northeast" "Unknown"

# ACOG Districts
mysterycall_assign_region(states, system = "acog")
#> [1] "District VIII" "District VII"  "District II"   "District IX"
#> [5] "District I"    "Unknown"

# AAO-HNS Districts
mysterycall_assign_region(states, system = "aao_hns")
#> [1] "District 7" "District 6" "District 2" "District 8"
#> [5] "District 1" "Unknown"

The function accepts: - Two-letter abbreviations ("CO", "TX") — case-insensitive, so "co" and "CO" both work. - Full state names ("Colorado", "Texas") — case-insensitive, so "colorado", "COLORADO", and "Colorado" all work. Internal title-casing logic handles common prepositions correctly (e.g., "district of columbia" maps correctly because "of" is preserved lowercase in the lookup key). - Mixed formats in the same vector — c("CO", "Texas", "new york") resolves correctly.

Applying to a roster

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    census_region = mysterycall_assign_region(addresses_state, system = "census"),
    acog_district = mysterycall_assign_region(addresses_state, system = "acog")
  )

# Summary table
table(roster$census_region, useNA = "always")

Handling territories and possessions

Puerto Rico ("PR"), Guam ("GU"), and the US Virgin Islands ("VI") are included in the ACOG and AAO-HNS maps but not in the Census Bureau regional map (they are not states and are not assigned to a Census region). They will return "Unknown" with system = "census". This is intentional and correct.

mysterycall_assign_region(c("PR", "GU", "VI"), system = "acog")
#> [1] "District IV" "Unknown"     "District IV"   # GU is AAO-HNS only

mysterycall_assign_region(c("PR", "GU", "VI"), system = "census")
#> [1] "Unknown" "Unknown" "Unknown"

5. Medical school classification

Why classify medical school?

Physician training background is a potential confounder in access studies. Internationally trained physicians (IMGs) are disproportionately located in rural and underserved areas and may have different scheduling practices. Osteopathic physicians (DOs) have a distinct training philosophy and may be distributed differently across specialties and geographic regions. Knowing whether a physician trained at a US allopathic school, a US osteopathic school, a Canadian school, or an international school helps you control for training-related heterogeneity.

The four classification categories

mysterycall_classify_medical_school() assigns one of four values:

Category Meaning
"US_MD" US allopathic medical school (LCME-accredited)
"US_DO" US osteopathic medical school (COCA-accredited)
"CAN_MD" Canadian medical school (LCMCC/AFMC member)
"IMG" International medical graduate (all other countries)

Classification priority is: US_DO > CAN_MD > IMG > US_MD. Schools that match no DO, Canadian, or international pattern are assumed to be US allopathic. This is the correct default because the LCME-accredited school list is far larger than the DO and Canadian lists, making a permissive default more practical than enumerating all 155 US allopathic schools.

Applying to a roster

The school name column in NPPES is typically found via the CMS Physician Compare or NPI registry enrichment workflow, not in the base NPPES download. It may be labelled medical_school_name, training_institution, or similar.

schools <- c(
  "University of Colorado School of Medicine",   # US_MD
  "Philadelphia College of Osteopathic Medicine",# US_DO: "osteopathic" matches DO list
  "McGill University Faculty of Medicine",        # CAN_MD: "mcgill" in Canadian list
  "Ross University School of Medicine",           # IMG: "ross university" in IMG list
  "Universidad Nacional Autonoma de Mexico",      # IMG: "universidad" matches IMG list
  NA_character_                                   # Unknown
)

mysterycall_classify_medical_school(schools)
#> [1] "US_MD"   "US_DO"   "CAN_MD"  "IMG"     "IMG"     "Unknown"

# Apply to a roster column
roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    med_school_type = mysterycall_classify_medical_school(medical_school_name)
  )

Intersection with credential column

An important nuance: a physician with a "DO" credential in NPPES who graduated from a US osteopathic school is classified as "US_DO". But a physician with an "MD" credential who graduated from a school matching the IMG pattern is classified as "IMG" — international medical graduates who obtained USMLE equivalence and a US medical license still practise with MD credentials in the US.

Do not substitute the credential column (basic_credential) for the medical school column. They measure different things. A physician can be credentialed as "MD" and still be an IMG if they obtained their degree from a foreign school.

# Correct: classify by school name; use credential only as a secondary check
roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    med_school_type = mysterycall_classify_medical_school(medical_school_name),
    # Flag potential mismatches: DO credential but school classified as US_MD
    credential_mismatch = (basic_credential == "DO") & (med_school_type == "US_MD")
  )

# If credential_mismatch > 0, investigate: school name may be missing or malformed
sum(roster$credential_mismatch, na.rm = TRUE)

6. Age imputation from graduation year

The NPPES age gap

The National Provider Identifier (NPI) registry does not publish physician birth year. This is a deliberate privacy protection. However, the registry does publish medical school graduation year, which is a strong proxy for age. mysterycall_impute_age() converts graduation year to estimated current age using the formula:

estimated_age = ref_year - grad_year + age_offset

where age_offset (default 27) is the median age at US medical school graduation. This method has been validated against Doximity birth-year data for over 32,000 OB/GYN physicians (r = 0.962, mean absolute error = 2.1 years) and 348 ENT physicians (r = 0.957, MAE = 1.7 years).

The ref_year parameter

Always pin ref_year to your study’s data collection year. Do not use the default (current calendar year) in a paper whose data were collected in a prior year. If your calls were made in 2023, use ref_year = 2023L. This ensures that the estimated ages in your analysis correspond to the physicians’ ages at the time they were called, not at the time you are writing the paper.

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    age_imputed = mysterycall_impute_age(
      graduation_year,
      ref_year   = 2023L,  # pin to your data collection year
      age_offset = 27L     # validated median age at US med school graduation
    )
  )

Warnings for implausible values

mysterycall_impute_age() issues a warning in two situations:

  1. Future graduation years (grad_year > ref_year): This almost always indicates a data entry error. For example, graduation_year = 20220 (a typo for 2022) will produce a future year warning. Investigate these before proceeding.

  2. Implausible ages (below min_age = 25 or above max_age = 90): Values outside this range are set to NA with a warning. A physician who graduated in 1924 would be computed as age 129 in 2023 — almost certainly a data entry error in NPPES. Check these rows manually.

# Example showing how warnings fire
mysterycall_impute_age(
  c(1980, 2010, 2028, 1920),  # 2028 is future; 1920 gives age > 90
  ref_year   = 2023L,
  age_offset = 27L
)
# Warning: 1 graduation year(s) exceed ref_year (2023) and will produce NA ages.
# Warning: 1 imputed age(s) outside [25, 90] set to NA. Check graduation years.
#> [1] 70 40 NA NA

Converting to decade brackets for Table 1

Raw imputed age is a continuous variable, which is appropriate for regression models. For Table 1, decade brackets are conventional. mysterycall_age_category() bins ages into standard brackets:

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    age_category = mysterycall_age_category(
      age_imputed,
      breaks    = c(30, 40, 50, 60, 70),  # creates 6 bins
      as_factor = TRUE   # ordered factor for correct table ordering
    )
  )

levels(roster$age_category)
#> [1] "<30"     "30-39"   "40-49"   "50-59"   "60-69"   "70+"     "Unknown"

table(roster$age_category, useNA = "always")

If you want non-standard age breaks — for example, to combine the youngest and oldest bins for a specialty where physicians under 35 are rare — supply custom breaks and labels:

roster <- roster |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    age_4cat = mysterycall_age_category(
      age_imputed,
      breaks = c(40, 50, 60),
      labels = c("<40", "40-49", "50-59", "60+"),
      as_factor = TRUE
    )
  )

The breaks vector defines the left boundaries of the intervals (exclusive lower bound). The rule is: a physician with age exactly 40 falls into the “40-49” category, not “<40”.

7. Gender backfill with Genderize.io

The NPPES gender gap

NPPES records physician sex in the basic_gender column as "M" or "F". However, approximately 5% of records are missing this field, either because the physician declined to provide it or because it was not required when their NPI was registered. Missing gender is not random — it is more common among older physicians who registered early in the NPPES era and among physicians with non-Western names.

mysterycall_genderize() fills in missing gender values by querying the Genderize.io API, which predicts gender from first names using a database of hundreds of millions of social media profiles. The API returns a predicted gender, a probability (0–1), and a name count (how many individuals with that name were in the database).

API rate limits and cost

The free tier of Genderize.io allows 1,000 unique name lookups per month. mysterycall_genderize() deduplicates names before querying, so if your roster has 300 providers but only 180 unique first names, it uses only 180 API credits. If you expect to run this step multiple times (e.g., during development), cache the output by specifying output_dir.

If you exceed the free tier, the API returns HTTP 429. mysterycall_genderize() catches this and raises an informative error rather than silently returning empty data. Paid tiers are available at https://genderize.io/#pricing.

The data_or_path parameter

mysterycall_genderize() accepts either a data frame or a file path to a CSV or Parquet roster. Your input must have a column named first_name. If NPPES data uses basic_first_name, rename it first:

roster_for_genderize <- roster |>
  dplyr::rename(first_name = basic_first_name)

Columns added by mysterycall_genderize():

Column Type Description
gender character "male", "female", or NA (ambiguous name)
probability double API confidence score, 0–1
count integer Number of individuals with that name in the Genderize.io database

Filtering by probability threshold

The API can predict gender for almost any name, but with widely varying confidence. A probability of 0.51 for a name means the API is essentially guessing. A common threshold for research use is 0.90:

# Load the cached genderized output
genderized <- readr::read_csv("data-processed/genderized/genderized_20231015120000_roster.csv")

# Apply a probability threshold before using the gender prediction
genderized <- genderized |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    gender_api = dplyr::if_else(
      probability >= 0.90,
      gender,
      NA_character_   # treat low-confidence predictions as missing
    )
  )

Combining Genderize.io with NPPES gender

Use Genderize.io only to fill in what NPPES is missing. Do not override NPPES basic_gender values with API predictions — the physician self-reported their gender to the registry, which is more authoritative than a name-based prediction.

genderized <- genderized |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    # Keep NPPES gender when available; fall back to Genderize.io when missing
    gender_combined = dplyr::case_when(
      basic_gender %in% c("M", "F")     ~ basic_gender,          # NPPES takes precedence
      probability >= 0.90 & !is.na(gender) ~ toupper(substr(gender, 1, 1)),  # "male" -> "M"
      TRUE                              ~ NA_character_
    )
  )

# Summarise coverage improvement
before <- mean(is.na(roster$basic_gender))
after  <- mean(is.na(genderized$gender_combined))
message(sprintf(
  "Gender coverage: %.1f%% missing before genderize; %.1f%% missing after.",
  before * 100, after * 100
))

8. Preparing Table 1 variables

Purpose of mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars()

Once you have run all the enrichment steps above, the resulting data frame contains a mix of raw NPPES values, API outputs, and custom-classified columns — often with inconsistent capitalisation and coding. mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars() is a convenience wrapper that standardises all demographic columns in a single call, producing consistently named output columns ready for Table 1.

It does four things: 1. Imputes age from graduation year (if grad_year_col is supplied and age_col is not). 2. Creates decade-bracket age categories. 3. Standardises gender to "Male" / "Female" / "Unknown". 4. Passes through practice setting and region columns under standard names.

All parameters

Parameter Default Purpose
age_col NULL Name of an existing age column; skips imputation if supplied
grad_year_col NULL Graduation year column for age imputation
gender_col NULL Raw gender column to standardise
setting_col NULL Practice setting column (passed through as setting_std)
region_col NULL Region column (passed through as region_std)
ref_year current year Reference year for age imputation — pin this to your data year

Gender standardisation and the binary warning

mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars() maps gender values to a binary classification: "Male", "Female", or "Unknown". The following input values are recognised:

Input value Output gender_std
"male", "m", "Male", "M" "Male"
"female", "f", "Female", "F" "Female"
NA, "", "unknown", anything else "Unknown"

Matching is case-insensitive and whitespace-trimmed. Non-binary and ambiguous values are silently bucketed into "Unknown" with a warning message. Before calling this function, inspect your gender column for unexpected values:

# Always inspect before standardising
table(roster$gender_combined, useNA = "always")
#>    F    M    NA
#>  142  155    3

# If you see anything unexpected (e.g., "non-binary", "unknown", "X"),
# decide how to handle it before calling prepare_table1_vars().

This is a deliberate design choice for mystery-caller research: studies typically report binary sex assigned at registry registration, not gender identity. If your study protocol requires tracking non-binary providers separately, do not use this wrapper — handle gender standardisation manually.

Complete example

analysis_ready <- genderized |>
  mysterycall_prepare_table1_vars(
    grad_year_col = "graduation_year",   # will compute age_imputed and age_category
    gender_col    = "gender_combined",   # will compute gender_std
    setting_col   = "practice_setting",  # will copy to setting_std
    region_col    = "census_region",     # will copy to region_std
    ref_year      = 2023L                # pin to data collection year
  )

# New columns added:
# age_imputed, age_category, gender_std, setting_std, region_std
dplyr::glimpse(analysis_ready |> dplyr::select(age_imputed, age_category,
                                                 gender_std, setting_std, region_std))

9. Stratified sampling

When you need a balanced pilot sample

Mystery-caller pilot studies often specify a target number of calls per subspecialty. If your full roster has 800 physicians but your IRB-approved protocol allows only 90 calls (30 per subspecialty), you need a balanced random sample. mysterycall_stratified_sample() provides this.

Parameters

Parameter Type Purpose
group_col character Column to stratify on (e.g., "subspecialty")
n_per_group integer Target rows per group
seed integer Random seed for reproducibility

Behaviour when a group is smaller than n_per_group

Groups with fewer rows than n_per_group are returned in full without upsampling. Upsampling (returning a group with replacement) would duplicate physician records, creating artificial inflation of the study denominator. If you have only 15 paediatric otolaryngologists in your roster and request n_per_group = 30, all 15 will be returned.

set.seed(42L)  # for documentation; pass seed= instead in production

pilot_roster <- mysterycall_stratified_sample(
  data        = roster,
  group_col   = "subspecialty",
  n_per_group = 30L,
  seed        = 42L
)

# Verify balance
table(pilot_roster$subspecialty)
#> General Otolaryngology       Head and Neck Oncology   Laryngology
#>                      30                           30          30

# Groups returned in full when smaller than n_per_group
nrow(pilot_roster)  # may be < 30 * n_groups if any group was small

Reproducibility note

Always specify seed when using this function in a paper. Without a fixed seed, every run of mysterycall_stratified_sample() will produce a different sample. Pin the seed to your IRB approval date or a meaningful constant and document it in your methods.

10. Checking for over-called physicians

Why duplicates matter

In a mystery-caller study, each physician should receive exactly the number of calls specified in the protocol (typically one call per insurance type in a multi-wave design). Data entry errors, system glitches, or human error can cause a physician to be called more times than intended. These over-called physicians must be identified before analysis: including extra calls would inflate apparent acceptance rates and introduce within-physician correlation that violates independence assumptions.

mysterycall_check_duplicates() returns a subset of the call log containing only physicians who have been called more than max_calls times.

Parameters and output

Parameter Default Purpose
id_col required Name of the physician identifier column (e.g., "npi")
max_calls 2L Maximum allowed calls per physician

The returned data frame: - Contains only flagged physicians (those exceeding max_calls). - Has an additional n_calls column showing how many times each physician appears. - Is sorted descending by n_calls (worst offenders first). - Has an attribute "n_flagged" with the count of unique flagged physicians.

flagged <- mysterycall_check_duplicates(
  call_log,
  id_col    = "npi",
  max_calls = 2L   # each physician should appear at most twice (one per insurance arm)
)

# How many unique physicians were over-called?
attr(flagged, "n_flagged")
#> [1] 4

# Inspect the worst offenders
head(flagged[, c("npi", "physician_name", "call_date", "insurance", "n_calls")])

Remediation options

Once you have identified over-called physicians, you have two options:

Option 1 — Keep the first N calls and discard extras: This is appropriate when the extra calls were genuine scheduling errors and you want to retain the valid calls.

# Keep only the first 2 calls per NPI (chronological order)
call_log_clean <- call_log |>
  dplyr::arrange(npi, call_date) |>
  dplyr::group_by(npi) |>
  dplyr::slice_head(n = 2L) |>
  dplyr::ungroup()

Option 2 — Exclude all calls for over-called physicians: This is appropriate when the extra calls may have introduced contamination (e.g., the physician or scheduler recognised the caller after the first call) and you cannot determine which calls are valid.

flagged_npis <- unique(flagged$npi)
call_log_clean <- dplyr::filter(call_log, !npi %in% flagged_npis)

message(sprintf(
  "Excluded %d physicians (%d calls) from analysis due to over-calling.",
  length(flagged_npis),
  sum(call_log$npi %in% flagged_npis)
))

Document your choice in the methods section and in the CONSORT flow diagram.
